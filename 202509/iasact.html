IASACT 2025: Food, Faith, and Culture

 

The 2025 Institute for Advanced Study in Asian Cultures and Theologies (IASACT), one of the United Board’s signature programs, drew to a close on July 19, 2025 at the Divinity School of Chung Chi College, CUHK. This year, 15 scholars from across Asia gathered for a month of ground-breaking research, vibrant conversations, and interdisciplinary exploration centered around the theme of “Asian Foodscapes”—with special focus on ethical food practices, sustainable systems, and the intersections of culture, religion, and community well-being.

 

IASACT’s magic appeared most vividly in the intersections of backgrounds and interests. For some participants, food had already shaped their academic journey. Dr. Min Su, an anthropologist from University Malaya and who is of “Hui” descent, has long focused on the intersections of food, identity, and religion. Her earlier work explored how food taboos among the Hui Muslim minority in China shape ethnic identity and connect daily practices to broader understandings of Islam and world religions.

 

Building on this foundation, her current research examines “audit cultures” in the context of durian production and trade across Asia, with particular attention to how state policies, enterprise standards, and grassroots practices intersect. She analyzes how food safety protocols, biosafety regulations, and packaging requirements govern the circulation of durian, a popular fruit in Asia, thereby revealing the complex layers of transnational regulation and cultural meaning surrounding this tropical commodity.

Others arrived at IASACT through winding intellectual paths. Dr. Ashok Mocherla, a sociologist from the Indian Institute of Technology Indore & Centre for Cross-cultural Studies in Chennai, India, whose research embraces religious minorities and Dalit Christian communities, observes: “Food is universal—everyone eats—but how, when, and with whom we eat varies across cultures. In India, debates over food draw strong religious and political elements, making the issue highly charged.” His first East Asian academic experience opened new doors for interdisciplinary and intercultural thinking.

Qudsia Firdous

Born in Dubai and later relocating to Pakistan, Dr. Qudsia Firdous’s academic path led her from aspirations in medicine to profound commitment to religious studies and spiritual healing. Her research and teaching at Riphah International University in Islamabad integrates Islamic studies and alternative medicine, and her projects reflect the belief that “religion is the ultimate tool for peace—if we want a peaceful world, we must root it in religion.” For Qudsia, the intersection of food culture and the te1achings of Islam provided fertile ground for her work and perspective as a scholar.

 

Ashok praises highly the structure and delivery of IASACT 2025. He highlights three qualities that make this program unique: (1) its genuine interdisciplinarity, bringing together scholars from diverse fields; (2) its methodological openness, welcoming not only academic papers but also policy studies or other project proposals; and (3) its focus on Asia by Asians, which reduces colonial misunderstanding that sometimes comes with Euro-American scholarship. Together, these qualities make the program valuable and highly relevant in his view.

 

Dr. David Johns, a seasoned academic and university leader who joined the program as external assessor, remarks on this atmosphere of mutual discovery and connectedness: “IASACT’s strength lies in its structure—space to pause, to think deeply, and to build relationships across institutions, faith traditions, and academic disciplines.” David praises how the program kindled friendships “that transcend borders” and revealed that academic work “can be both deeply contextual and broadly relevant.”
David Johns

IASACT 2025 thus stands as a powerful affirmation of the United Board’s commitment to nurturing community, fostering innovative scholarship, and supporting and advancing cultural and theological education and research.
